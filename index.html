Projects

Things I've built.

01
CutList Optimiser ↗
MaxCut is a well-known commercial cutting optimisation tool. A bin-packing solver that takes panel dimensions and computes optimal cut layouts to minimise waste, with printable PDF output.

The hard part wasn't the algorithm. It was the constraint that real wood panels have grain direction, saw blade kerf, and minimum offcut sizes. The solver needed to model the physical reality, not just the maths.
What I learned

Optimisation problems look simple on paper. Translating physical constraints like kerf width, grain direction, and minimum usable offcuts into solver constraints is where most of the real work lives. OR-Tools is powerful but unforgiving. It will find every edge case in your model.

02
LRU Cache ↗
Least Recently Used cache built from scratch. A doubly linked list and hashmap combined to hit O(1) get and put. A deliberate deep-dive into data structures rather than reaching for a library.

The challenge was keeping the two structures in sync without O(n) lookups on eviction. Getting that wrong is easy; debugging it when it goes wrong is educational.
What I learned

Pointer management matters enormously. A single incorrect reference in the doubly linked list produces bugs that are silent until they're catastrophic. Building this made LeetCode-style DS questions feel genuinely answerable rather than academic.

03
Allegra the Barman · CPU Scheduler ↗
CPU scheduling simulator implementing FCFS, SJF, Priority, and MLFQ. Includes real experimental data collection and performance comparison across scheduling strategies. The name comes from the analogy of a bartender deciding which order to serve next.

Writing MLFQ was the steepest part. The queue demotion logic had to handle starvation prevention, boosting, and priority aging simultaneously, all in C with manual memory management.
What I learned

OS concepts only become real when you implement them. MLFQ looks elegant in a textbook. In C, it's a reminder that every design decision has a cost, and that concurrency and shared state are genuinely hard to reason about.

AI-assisted tool to help teachers mark student submissions faster. Sponsored semester project at UCT, built in a team of 5 under Mr Charles Mayosi. The system flags, categorises, and drafts feedback on submissions, cutting time spent per script without removing the human from the loop.

Semester Project · UCT · 2026
AI-Assisted Marking System

Teachers spend hours marking the same types of student responses. This tool uses AI to flag, categorise, and draft feedback on submissions, cutting time per script without removing the human in the loop.

The mission was simple: give teachers their evenings back.

Built under the sponsorship of Mr Charles Mayosi at UCT, the project gave us a real brief, real accountability, and a real stakeholder to present to. Not just a tutorial exercise. We had to manage scope, shifting requirements, and five people with different strengths who all needed to contribute meaningfully.

On working in a team

Five people building one thing sounds simple. It isn't. We had different strengths, different working rhythms, and one deadline. What held it together was clear ownership. Every component had a name behind it, and the discipline to communicate when something wasn't working rather than quietly working around each other. The best thing a team can do is make disagreement safe. We got better at that as the semester went on.

Origin story

How I got here.

The SparkEarly curiosity

It started with wanting to invent things. Not software specifically. Just the feeling that things could be different, better, built differently. The idea that one person with the right tools and enough stubbornness could change something real.

I didn't have the vocabulary for it yet. But the aspiration was there long before the laptop was.

Arduino EraFirst hardware steps

My entry point into building things was Arduino. Microcontrollers. Hardware that blinked, buzzed, read sensors, and sometimes just refused to work for reasons I couldn't explain at 1am.

I'd research components online and ask my parents to help me buy them: resistors, LEDs, ultrasonic sensors, motor drivers. They didn't always know what any of it was for. They bought it anyway. That meant something.

Arduino taught me that the gap between an idea and a working thing is mostly friction, not impossibility. You just have to be stubborn enough to sit with the friction.

GamingSystems thinking in disguise

I've always gamed. But somewhere along the way I stopped just playing and started watching how games were built: the economy systems, the AI decision-making, the way level design nudges you without telling you. Games are some of the most complex interactive systems ever made, and they're designed to feel effortless. That's not an accident.

Gaming gave me a way to think about systems, feedback loops, and user experience long before I had names for any of those things.

CompSci at UCTThe umbrella opened

When I got into Computer Science at UCT, it felt like the Arduino experiments and the gaming and the vague want-to-invent-things suddenly had a formal home. CS is the umbrella that covers all of it: algorithms, systems, AI, hardware, software, the web, security. I enrolled wanting to explore everything under it.

Adding Business Computing wasn't an afterthought. Software built without understanding the world it enters is incomplete. I wanted both sides.

What anime taught me

Lessons from the shows I've watched.

Yes, genuinely. No apologies. The best anime doesn't just entertain. It teaches you things about persistence and belief that are harder to say out loud.

—

You don't quit when it gets hard. You quit when you stop caring.

Every shonen protagonist loses. Badly, repeatedly, and publicly. The lesson isn't that they always win. It's that they get back up every single time. That's the whole point. I've taken that seriously. When a bug takes three days, when a project isn't going well, when I don't understand something, I don't quit. I adjust and continue.

—

It takes just one person believing in you to know that you can.

Self-belief matters. But there's something that lands differently when someone else sees what you're capable of before you fully see it yourself. A mentor, a parent, a teacher who picks you for something. That moment of external belief can unlock something internal that couldn't get there alone. I know this because it happened to me. My family has always been that person. This lessson ties to the very first episode of My Hero Academia.

—

The strongest people carry others, not just themselves.

The characters I respect most aren't the most individually powerful, characters like Deku, Kuroku, Takemichi. They're the ones who bring others with them. Being good at something is the floor. Using it to lift the people around you is the ceiling. That's what tutoring is. That's what the Leo Library was. That's the kind of engineer I want to be.
